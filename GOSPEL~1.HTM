01

A story of formation, not money

Consumerism is a training program promising endless satisfaction through instant gratification. It teaches us to solve discomfort by spending and to build identity from what we own.

02

The universal catechist

TV ownership exploded from 9% of US homes in 1950 to 87% in 1960, teaching everyone to evaluate life by comparison. Credit cards removed the brake on consumption.

9% to 87% TV in one decade, Visa launched 1958
03

The life cycle is broken

Every birth cohort after 1945 broke the old pattern of returning to church with age. The Silent Generation rose from 33% to 47% weekly attendance; later cohorts start lower and stay flat.

Silent Gen +14 points, Millennials may still be falling
04

From creators to consumers

We changed the verb of Christianity from create to consume. The church became a product you attend instead of a vocation you inhabit. You hear it in 'I am just not getting fed.'

05

Why missionaries quit

It is neurological, not only spiritual. The dopamine loop craves novelty; missions is slow. Liquid modernity makes commitment feel like a burden, and endless choice makes closing doors terrifying.

5.1% leave yearly, 71% of departures preventable
06

The Global South inversion

Africa went from under 10 million Christians in 1900 to 734 million today. Not because poverty is virtuous, but because the comparison engine had not yet saturated. They still know how to stay.

under 10 million in 1900 to 734 million today
07

Sending shackled soldiers

The average American carried $104,215 in debt. We are imprisoned before we hear the call, and we risk exporting the very disease that killed us. The fix: build do not attend, run a dopamine audit, screen for identity.

$104,215 average debt

“You cannot serve both the Gospel of Christ and the Gospel of Consumption.”

“We are sending soldiers into battle who are already shackled.”

The unreached hearts may be the senders'. Can we form disciples who are not consumers first?
