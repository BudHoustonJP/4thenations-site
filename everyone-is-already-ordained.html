The Great Commission is not a staffing problem. It is a priesthood that forgot it exists.

We keep framing the Great Commission as a staffing problem. Not enough goers. Not enough senders. Not enough funded units to cover the frontier. So we recruit harder, mobilize louder, and the math still does not close.

Two things are wrong with that math. The target is not standing still, and we are counting the wrong workforce. Scripture already ordained the many, and most of them do not know it. Scroll through, and work the controls.

The workforce we forgot

We are counting the ordained few. Scripture ordained the many.

Under the Old Covenant a credentialed class stood between God and the people. The cross dissolved that into a royal priesthood of all believers (1 Peter 2:9). Flip between the two models and watch the ceiling move.

The ceiling

A professional class carries the gospel

A specialized class does the sacred work. Everyone else funds it, attends it, and applauds it. That made sense when access to God ran through a credentialed few. It is a liability now, because it caps the workforce at exactly the point the task requires it to expand. There will never be enough professional goers to cover 7,000-plus frontier peoples across the nodes they now occupy.

The delivery mechanism

Every believer is a consecrated priest

Under the New Covenant every person in Christ was consecrated for priestly work. The workforce is not concentrated inside sending agencies. It is already distributed across labor corridors and gateway cities. Peter did not write "royal priesthood" to a seminary. He wrote it to scattered exiles (1 Peter 1:1). The first people told they were a priesthood were a diaspora.

The available workforce, under this model
The bottleneck is theological, not logistical. The frontier is not short on people. It is short on believers who know they were ordained to reach it.
Exodus 29, element for element

What consecrated Aaron now consecrates you

The ceremony that set apart Aaron and his sons maps onto what happens to every believer. Open each step of the ordination.

They did not wash themselves. This is regeneration, the washing of rebirth by the Spirit (Titus 3:5). No one qualifies for priestly work in their natural state.

The believer is clothed in the righteousness of Christ (Galatians 3:27), serving not in personal merit but in imputed standing.

The ancient sign of the Spirit, once reserved for kings, prophets, and priests, is no longer rationed to a select few. It rests on every believer (1 John 2:20; 2 Corinthians 1:21-22).

The sharpest image

The blood touched the ear, the thumb, and the toe. Not the résumé.

Moses put the blood of ordination on three places (Exodus 29:20): the right ear, the right thumb, the right big toe. What you hear, what you do, where you go. Claim all three. Hold back one and the sacrifice is not yet whole (Romans 12:1).

For your team

Three questions before you recruit one more worker

1

What share of your mobilization strategy assumes the workforce must be recruited and sent, versus awakened where it already lives?

2

Are there believers already present in your target nodes, through diaspora, business, or study, counted in your strategy, or invisible to it?

3

If every member of your sending church is an ordained priest, what changes about how you disciple them long before anyone talks about going?
