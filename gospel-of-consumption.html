Catechized

A story of formation, not money

Consumerism is a training program. It promises endless satisfaction through instant gratification, teaching us to solve discomfort by spending and to build identity from what we own.

The universal catechist

Television ownership exploded from 9 percent of US homes in 1950 to 87 percent in 1960. Credit cards removed the last brake on spending: BankAmericard launched in 1958, Barclaycard in 1966.

the break point

The life cycle broke

For generations, people drifted from church as young adults and returned with age. Every birth cohort after 1945 broke that pattern. The Silent Generation climbed to 47 percent weekly attendance. Every cohort since starts lower and stays flatter.

Consume

From creators to consumers

We changed the verb of Christianity from create to consume. The church became a product you attend instead of a vocation you inhabit. You can hear it in the line, "I am just not getting fed."

Why missionaries quit

It is neurological, not only spiritual. The dopamine loop craves novelty; missions is slow and un-novel. Liquid modernity makes commitment feel like a burden, and the paradox of choice makes closing doors terrifying. Of those who leave, 71 percent go for preventable reasons.

The Global South inversion

Africa went from under 10 million Christians in 1900 to 734 million today. Not because poverty is virtuous, but because the comparison engine had not yet saturated. They still know how to stay.

Sending shackled soldiers

We are imprisoned before we ever hear the call, and we risk exporting the very disease that killed us. The fix: build, do not attend; run a dopamine audit; screen senders for identity stability before deployment.

“You cannot serve both the Gospel of Christ and the Gospel of Consumption.”

“We are sending soldiers into battle who are already shackled.”

The unreached hearts may be the senders'.
