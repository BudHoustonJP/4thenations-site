A Western worker with a seminary degree and an airtight case for the resurrection can spend ten years in a folk-Islamic village and watch nothing move. Then a child gets healed after prayer, and the whole household wants to know who Jesus is.

We read that as a miracle. It is also a diagnosis. The church carries three things into every field: power, truth, and love. In any given worldview, one of them breaks ground first and opens the way for the other two. Lead with the right one and the gospel walks in. Lead with the wrong one and you knock for a decade on a door nobody uses. Scroll through, and work the controls.

Which one goes first

The same gospel needs a different point of entry

Reading the map tells you where a person is standing. It does not tell you how to move them. Pick a worldview and see which door tends to break ground, and where the Western default of leading with truth misfires.

Lead with power
The daily question: who is stronger?

Folk religion, animism, and most popular-level Islam and Hinduism live in a world of spirits, curses, ancestors, and unseen forces. Power here is not only dramatic healing or exorcism. It is release from crippling fear, freedom from addiction, the breaking of generational bondage.

Paul Hiebert named the Western blind spot here the flaw of the excluded middle: we bring high theology and empirical facts and skip the realm where these people actually live. Truth lands only after power has answered the question they are actually asking.

Door that breaks ground: Power
Lead with truth
The daily question: is it true?

Modern, literate, argument-respecting contexts, including much of the secular West until recently and large parts of the educated global church. Here declarative persuasion still works. The case for the resurrection, the coherence of the Christian story, the historical evidence: these open the door.

This is the one door Western workers know how to use. So we use it everywhere, and outside it we answer questions nobody is asking.

Door that breaks ground: Truth
Lead with love
The daily question: why trust the category at all?

The secular, post-Christian West, and increasingly the global cities its culture exports to. These people have heard the propositions and filed them under things religious people claim. Another argument bounces off, because the problem is not the evidence. It is that they no longer trust the category.

What breaks through is a long demonstration of love: meals dropped off during a divorce, a standing seat at the table, the same people still showing up three years later. Embodied, costly, and credible, not a sharper syllogism.

Door that breaks ground: Love

What Jesus refused to do

Lead with one, but never amputate the other two

The moment you lead with one, you are tempted to abandon the rest. That is how movements go wrong. Open each boundary to see where Jesus drew the line, and what the avenue collapses into when it is cut loose.

Jesus refused to use power as spectacle or proof on demand. In the wilderness he would not turn stones to bread or throw himself from the temple to force a sign (Matt 4). When Herod wanted a miracle for entertainment, Jesus gave him silence (Luke 23:8-9). He healed constantly, then told people to keep quiet about it (Mark 1:44).

Cut loose from truth, power drifts into mysticism. A circus.

Jesus told hard truth, but he refused to weaponize it. He looked at the rich young ruler, loved him, and only then named the one thing he lacked (Mark 10:21). With Nicodemus he gave a respected Pharisee the hardest truth in a patient, private dialogue aimed at rescue rather than condemnation (John 3:17). And he kept truth to the right timing: you cannot bear them now (John 16:12).

Cut loose from love, truth hardens into legalism. A club.

Love never collapsed into mere affirmation. He loved the rich young ruler and still let him leave grieving. He loved Peter and still said get behind me, Satan (Matt 16:23). He made a whip and cleared the temple because love for the Father's house and for the exploited demanded confrontation (John 2). At the well, his love looked like naming her five husbands, not avoiding the subject (John 4).

Cut loose from truth, love collapses into sociology. Sentiment.
