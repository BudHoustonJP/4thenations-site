Assumptions, model notes, and sources

What this is. A gospel-burden model. It sizes the annual evangelistic need by counting every event that creates a need for a new believer: births (all counted as lost), non-believer net in-migration, and deaths. It is not a net population balance.

Gospel burden vs net floor. Gospel burden counts all deaths in full. Net floor subtracts the lost who die, since they also leave the pool, giving a conservative lower bound. The honest answer sits between the two.

The migration lever. Migration is a rate per 1,000 so it scales with population. International-heavy migration lowers the in-migrant evangelical share, which raises the figure.

The reach goal. Separate from the keep-even number, this solves for the baptisms per day to move the evangelical share to the chosen target over the chosen horizon, accounting for population growth, migration, and believer deaths. A reach target turns off once the metro is already above it; most US metros are above 2% and 10% evangelical, so the increase goals are usually the live ones.

Definitions matter. Evangelical share here is self-identified, not practicing. State the definition whenever you cite a number from this tool.

City evangelical estimates. Per-metro shares are starting estimates anchored to Pew's 2023-24 Religious Landscape Study, which reports evangelical share for 34 large metros. Edit any value. For precise figures use the Pew RLS metro pages.

Sources. Metro populations: US Census Bureau 2024 and Statistics Canada 2024 estimates. Evangelical shares: Pew Research Center 2023-24 Religious Landscape Study. Births: March of Dimes PeriStats. Baptism benchmark: Lifeway Research 2024 Annual Church Profile. Figures are estimates for strategy and mobilization, rounded for display.
